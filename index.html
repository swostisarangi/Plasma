Introduction

Galaxies like the Milky way have large magnetic fields with significant energy densities. Dynamo theories try explaining the processes underpinning the amplification and persistence of small seed magnetic fields within a galaxy. Employing galactic dynamo simulations, we can, thus,investigate the intricate processes underlying their generation and sustenance. In order to accurately predict the behaviour of such galactic processes, we need to account for certain factors such as:

  • Characterisitics of the Magnetic Field which factors in the interactions of the conducting fluids within the galaxy
  • Rotation of the galactic plane which generates the so called $\alpha-\Omega$ effect
  • Turbulence within the conducting fluids which happens to be an important mechanism for the amplification of the seed magnetic field

Mean Field Dynamo Equations

Assuming the $\alpha$-effect and turbulent diffusivity to be represented by scalar quantities, the mean field dynamo equations reduce to the following simple form: $$ \frac{\partial \overline{\boldsymbol{B}}}{\partial t}=\nabla \times(\overline{\boldsymbol{V}} \times \overline{\boldsymbol{B}})+\nabla \times(\alpha \overline{\boldsymbol{B}})-\nabla \times \beta \nabla \times \overline{\boldsymbol{B}} $$ If $\beta$ is independent of position, $\nabla \times \beta \nabla \times \overline{B} = \beta \nabla^{2}\overline{B}$ since $\nabla \cdot \overline{B}=0.$
We will be working in the cylindrical coordinate system since we are assuming our galaxy to be an axially symmetric thin disk. Thus, $\vec{B} = (B_r,B_\phi,B_z)$. Thus, the mean field dynamo equations for an axially symmetric thin disc are as follows: \begin{align} &\frac{\partial B_{r}}{\partial t} =-\frac{\partial}{\partial z}(\alpha\overline{B}_{\phi})+\beta\frac{\partial^{2}B_{r}}{\partial z^{2}}+\beta\frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left[\frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial}{\partial r}(r\overline{B}_{r})\right], \\ &\frac{\partial\overline{B}_{\phi}}{\partial t} =S\overline{B}_{r}+r\frac{\partial\Omega}{\partial z}\overline{B}_{z}+\frac{\partial}{\partial z}(\alpha\overline{B}_{r})-\frac{\partial}{\partial r}(\alpha\overline{B}_{z})+\beta\frac{\partial^{2}\overline{B}_{\phi}}{\partial z^{2}}+\beta\frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left[\frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial}{\partial r}(r\overline{B}_{\phi})\right] \\ & \\ &\frac{\partial\overline{B}_{z}}{\partial t} =\frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial}{\partial r}(r\alpha\overline{B}_{\phi})+\beta\frac{\partial^{2}\overline{B}_{z}}{\partial z^{2}}+\beta\frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left[\frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial}{\partial r}(r\overline{B}_{z})\right], \end{align} where, S is the shear velocity rate.
Neglecting the radial derivatives and the $B_z$ term completely under the thin disk approximation, we have: \begin{aligned} &\frac{\partial\overline{B}_{r}}{\partial t}=-\frac{\partial}{\partial z}(\alpha\overline{B}_{\phi})+\frac{\partial^{2}\overline{B}_{r}}{\partial z^{2}},\\&\frac{\partial\overline{B}_\phi}{\partial t}=DS\overline{B}_r+\frac{\partial^2\overline{B}_\phi}{\partial z^2}, \end{aligned} where, $\alpha$ and S can in general depend on r.

Neglecting the $\alpha-\Omega$ effects: Diffusion Equation

Neglecting the alpha effect and the stretching term $\vec{v} \times \vec{B}$, we have: \begin{equation} \frac{\partial\bar{\mathbf{B}}}{\partial t}=\eta\nabla^{2}\bar{\mathbf{B}} \end{equation} Thus, the $B_r$ and $B_\phi$ components evolves as follows: \begin{equation} \frac{\partial B_r}{\partial\tilde{t}}=\left(\frac{\partial^2B_r}{\partial\tilde{z}^2}\right) \end{equation} \begin{equation} \frac{\partial B_\phi}{\partial\tilde{t}}=\left(\frac{\partial^2B_\phi}{\partial\tilde{z}^2}\right) \end{equation}

Runge-Kutta Method

The Algorithm

It is by far the most popular algorithm to solve any system of coupled differential equations. Despite being primarily based on Taylor expansion, Runge-Kutta (RK) approaches typically yield better algorithms for solving ODEs with the same step size and stability. Actually, it's a generalisation of the Predictor-Corrector and Euler approaches. Consider the following: \begin{align} \frac{d y}{d x} & =f(y(x), x) \\ \int_{y_n}^{y_n+h} d y & =\int_{x_n}^{x_n+h} f(y(x), x) d x \\ y\left(x_n+h\right) & =y\left(x_n\right)+\int_{x_n}^{x_n+h} f(y(x), x) d x \end{align} Let's use the midpoint method to estimate the above integral. \begin{align} \bar{x}_n & =\frac{\left(x_n+h\right)+x_n}{2}=x_n+\frac{h}{2} \\ y\left(x_n+h\right) & =y\left(x_n\right)+h f\left(y\left(x_n+h / 2\right), x_n+h / 2\right)+\mathcal{O}\left(h^3\right) \end{align} Since $y\left(x_n+h / 2\right)$ is unknown, we use the Forward Euler to approximate it: $$ y\left(x_n+h / 2\right)=y\left(x_n\right)+\frac{h}{2} f\left(y\left(x_n\right), x_n\right) $$ From here, we obtain the second order Runge-Kutta (RK2) as follows: \begin{align} \kappa_1 & =h f\left(y\left(x_n\right), x_n\right) \\ \kappa_2 & =h f\left(y\left(x_n\right)+\kappa_1 / 2, x_n+h / 2\right) \\ y\left(x_n+h\right) & \approx y\left(x_n\right)+\kappa_2+\mathcal{O}\left(h^3\right) \end{align} Using the Simpson rule to estimate the integral, we will get the fourth order Runge-Kutta where we define: \begin{align} & \kappa_1=h f\left(y\left(x_n\right), x_n\right) \\ & \kappa_2=h f\left(y\left(x_n\right)+\kappa_1 / 2, x_n+h / 2\right) \\ & \kappa_3=h f\left(y\left(x_n\right)+\kappa_2 / 2, x_n+h / 2\right) \\ & \kappa_4=h f\left(y\left(x_n\right)+\kappa_3, x_n+h\right) \end{align} which when combined, yield us: \begin{equation} y\left(x_n+h\right)=y\left(x_n\right)+\frac{1}{6}\left(\kappa_1+2 \kappa_2+2 \kappa_3+\kappa_4\right)+\mathcal{O}\left(h^5\right) \end{equation}.

Truncation Error

The error accummulated at every successive stage of the iterated algorithm if referred to as the truncation error. In the case of RK4, it will scale as $h^5$ and the error can thus be written as $Ch^5$, where C is a constant which is independent of dt but is dependent on the starting time $t_0$. Therefore, for the estimation of the truncation error we would assume that C remains constant over the interval $[t_0,t_0 + h]$.
Let u be the approximate solution to x(t) at t0 + h obtained by carrying out a one-step fourth order Runge-Kutta approximation: $$x(t)=u+Ch^5$$ Let v be the approximate solution to x(t) at t0 + h obtained by carrying out a two-step fourth order Runge-Kutta approximation (with step sizes of h/2) $$ x(t)=v +2C\left(\frac{h}{2}\right)^5$$ Substracting these two equations we obtain: $$0=u-v+C\left(1-2^{-4}\right)h^{5}$$ from which we get the local truncation error as: $$Error = Ch^{5}={\frac{u-v}{1-h^{-4}}}\approx u-v$$ There are adaptive algorithms which monitor this error at every computational step and accordingly adjust h to minimise the local truncation error.

Defining the mesh and the spatial derivative

I will be solving the diffusion equation by discretising the spatial and temporal dimensions. Let's look at the simple 1-D diffusion equation to understand the approach: $$ \frac{\partial B}{\partial t} = \eta \frac{\partial^2 B}{\partial t^2}$$ where, $\eta$ is defined as the diffusivity parameter which is a scalar in our case. \ Discretising the spatial dimension using finite differencing, we get: $$\frac{\partial B}{\partial t} = \eta \frac{B_{i+1} - 2B_i + B_{i-1}}{(\delta x)^2}$$ Using the notion of a derivative for the temporal dimension, we have: $$\frac{B_{i}(t+\delta t) - B_{i}(t)}{\delta t} = \eta \frac{B_{i+1} - 2B_i + B_{i-1}}{(\delta x)^2}$$ $$\implies B_{i}(t+\delta t) = B_{i}(t) + \frac{\eta \delta t}{(\delta x)^2} (B_{i+1} - 2B_i + B_{i-1})$$ It is important to remember the Courant-Friedrichs-Lewy (CFL) condition while defining the mesh parameters. The CFL conditions states that: $$\frac{\delta x}{\delta t} < c$$ where, c is the speed of light which can be taken to be equal to 1.

def f1(u,v,dx,t,z):
    global eta
    d1 = np.zeros_like(u)
    d2 = np.zeros_like(v)
    for i in range(1,len(u)-1):
        d1[i] = alpha(z[i])*eta*(v[i+1] - v[i-1])/(2*dx)
        d2[i] = eta*(u[i - 1] - 2 * u[i] + u[i + 1])/dx**2
    return (-d1+d2)

def f2(u,v,dx,t,D):

def RK4_coupled(u,v,t,dx,dt,f1,f2,z,D):
    k1 = dt*f1(u,v,dx,t,z)
    l1 = dt*f2(u,v,dx,t,D)
    k2 = dt*f1(u+k1*0.5,v+l1*0.5,dx,t+dt/2,z)
    l2 = dt*f2(u+k1*0.5,v+l1*0.5,dx,t+dt/2,D)
    k3 = dt*f1(u+k2*0.5,v+l2*0.5,dx,t+dt/2,z)
    l3 = dt*f2(u+k2*0.5,v+l2*0.5,dx,t+dt/2,D)
    k4 = dt*f1(u+k3,v+l3,dx,t+dt/2,z)
    l4 = dt*f2(u+k3,v+l3,dx,t+dt/2,D)
    
    k = (1/6)*(k1+2*k2+2*k3+k4)
    l = (1/6)*(l1+2*l2+2*l3+l4)
    
    u_new = u + k
    v_new = v + l
    
    return u_new,v_new

This time we were lucky to use a value of D which happens to give us an exponentially increasing solution but that doesn't always happen. If we choose a D which is lower than a critical dynamo number $D_0$, our solution wouldn't converge. For example, D = -0.1 doesn't work out. So, we need a prescription to determine the critical dynamo number. The prescription reads as follows: keeping all other parameters constant, we vary D over a range of values and observe how the solutions emerge. The smallest value of D above which every solution converges is the critical value.
Now, in order to achieve this, I will solve the coupled differential equations for a range of D's and fit each solution with the decay equation and determine the decay constant. To determine the decay constant, I would simply convert the mid-plane solutions to log scale and fit a linear curve. The slope would yield me the decay constant.

    bp[0,:] = 0
    bp[-1,:] = 0

    # Incorporating the Initial Conditions
    br[:,0] = initial_br(z)
    bp[:,0] = initial_bp(z)
    # Iteratively Solving the DE
    for i in tqdm(range(1,M-1)):
        u = br[:,i-1]
        v = bp[:,i-1]
        next_t = t[i]
        br[:,i] = f(u,next_t,h,k,x_der)
        bp[:,i] = f(v,next_t,h,k,x_der)
    return br,bp

def RK2_coupled(u,v,t,dx,dt,f1,f2,z,D):
    k1 = dt*f1(u,v,dx,t,z)
    l1 = dt*f2(u,v,dx,t,D)
    k2 = dt*f1(u+k1*0.5,v+l1*0.5,dx,t+dt/2,z)
    l2 = dt*f2(u+k1*0.5,v+l1*0.5,dx,t+dt/2,D)
    
    u_new = u + k2
    v_new = v + l2
    
    return u_new,v_new

def RK3_coupled(u,v,t,dx,dt,f1,f2,z,D):
    k1 = dt*f1(u,v,dx,t,z)
    l1 = dt*f2(u,v,dx,t,D)
    k2 = dt*f1(u+k1*0.5,v+l1*0.5,dx,t+dt/2,z)
    l2 = dt*f2(u+k1*0.5,v+l1*0.5,dx,t+dt/2,D)
    k3 = dt*f1(u+2*k2-k1,v+2*l2-l1,dx,t+dt/2,z)

    return u_new,v_new

In the simulation neglecting the effect, we saw an interesting feature that the pitch angle slowly evolved to a saturated value. This is so because the solutions are independent of each other and the proportionality essentially crops out of the initial seed field I plug into each of them. If I had plugged in the same field to both, we would expect the pitch angle to not vary at all!
As evidenced by the spatial variation of B evolving over time, I saw that below a certain critical value of the Dynamo number, the magnetic field amplification stays relatively small or even shows a decaying character! Above this critical dynamo number, we see that the amplification is humungous! It grows exponentially with time and closely resembles the analytical solution to this coupled system of equations.
